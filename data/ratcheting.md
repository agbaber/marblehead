# Is budget ratcheting visible in Marblehead's history?

"Ratcheting" is a concern that comes up a lot in override debates. The idea: once voters pass an override, the town's spending ceiling never resets. Each override becomes part of the permanent baseline. Future budgets compound on top, and the town ends up locked into a higher level of spending forever.

That's the worry. Does the Marblehead record support it?

## The short answer

Marblehead hasn't passed an operating override in 20 years. The last one was in 2005. Every one since (2011, 2022, 2023, 2025) failed at the ballot.

If ratcheting were the mechanism creating Marblehead's current budget pressure, those 20 override-free years should have seen the pressure ease. No new overrides means no new ratchet clicks; the existing baseline would just keep compounding at the 2.5% cap plus new growth, bounded and predictable.

That is not what happened. Over those 20 years, spending grew faster than the statutory revenue ceiling, and today's projected $8 million structural deficit was rebuilt from a roughly-balanced starting point in FY07. None of that $8 million came from new overrides. There were none to come from.

So the thing pushing Marblehead's budget out of balance is not "we keep adding to the base through overrides." It has to be something else. Group-insurance premiums, out-of-district <abbr class="g" title="Special Education">SPED</abbr> tuition, collective-bargaining agreements, and state/federal mandates all grow at rates the town does not set. They are the usual suspects, and the data is consistent with them.

The ratcheting mechanism is real but small. The overrides that passed in the early 2000s added about $8 million of permanent authority to today's $77.5 million ceiling (roughly 11%). That share has been stable for two decades. It is not the cause of the current pressure.

## Marblehead's operating override history

Four successful operating overrides, all FY02 through FY06, adding **$4.92 million** in permanent levy authority.<sup class="cite"
  data-href="https://dls-gw.dor.state.ma.us/reports/rdpage.aspx?rdreport=votes.prop2_5.overrideunderride"
  data-source="MA DOR, Proposition 2½ Override/Underride Database"></sup>

| FY effective | Vote date | Amount | Purpose on ballot |
|---|---|---|---|
| FY02 | 2001-06-18 | $300,000 | Sewers and surface drains |
| FY04 | 2003-06-16 | $1,381,017 | Supplemental budget expenses |
| FY05 | 2004-06-21 | $512,397 | Schools, waste collection, library |
| FY06 | 2005-06-15 | $2,730,167 | Several town departments |

Nothing since. Debt exclusions (which sunset when the underlying bond is paid off, and do not compound into the operating base) have continued to pass, but they are outside the ratcheting question.

Reading the chart:

- Both lines start at 100 in FY06.
- Both end up above 150 by FY19, because both grew.
- The revenue ceiling grew 56%. School spending grew 66%. Spending pulled ahead by about 10 index points over 13 years.
- None of that spending pull-ahead came from a new override. There were none in this window.

Ten index points over 13 years is the equivalent of about half a percentage point per year of extra spending growth. It sounds small. But compounded on a $41 million FY07 base for 20 years, half a percentage point per year produces about $8 million of accumulated gap by FY27.<sup class="cite"
  data-href="https://github.com/agbaber/marblehead/blob/main/data/master_financial_data_FY01-24.csv"
  data-source="Derivation: $41M FY07 general fund education base; 3.45%/yr revenue growth vs. 3.98%/yr education growth compounded over 20 years produces an $8M gap. Underlying CAGRs computed from data/master_financial_data_FY01-24.csv and data/marblehead_levy.csv."></sup> Eight million dollars is roughly the size of the structural deficit the town is currently projecting for FY27.

That is how a seemingly-balanced budget ends up structurally out of balance over a generation, without any overrides happening along the way.

## What ratcheting did contribute

The early-2000s overrides added $4.92 million in permanent levy authority. That amount has compounded at the levy-limit growth rate (the 2.5% cap plus certified new growth) ever since. Compounded at 2.5% alone for 21 years, it grows to about $8.3 million of embedded authority in the FY27 $77.5 million ceiling,<sup class="cite"
  data-href="https://github.com/agbaber/marblehead/blob/main/data/marblehead_levy.csv"
  data-source="Marblehead levy limit FY00-FY27 (data/marblehead_levy.csv); $4.92M compounded at 2.5%/yr for 21 years"></sup> or about 11%.

That is the real ratcheting contribution. It is genuine, and it is exactly what Proposition 2&frac12; says an override does. But it has been in the base since 2006. It is not what is pushing the gap wider today. The gap is being pushed wider by expenses growing faster than the ceiling grows, which is a different mechanism entirely.

## Which peer comparison you use matters

The peer list in `data/peer_override_history.csv` (Arlington, Brookline, Hingham, Lexington, Melrose, Natick, Needham, Stoneham, Wellesley, Winchester) shows Marblehead as the lone override holdout. Every one of those towns has passed at least two operating overrides since FY07.

A different wealthy-suburb cohort looks very different:

| Dover | 21 |
| Marblehead | 21 |

Source: MA DOR override database, applied across all 255 Massachusetts towns with any override win on record.<sup class="cite"
  data-href="https://dls-gw.dor.state.ma.us/reports/rdpage.aspx?rdreport=votes.prop2_5.overrideunderride"
  data-source="MA DOR, Proposition 2½ Override/Underride Database; aggregated in data/dor_override_history_all.csv"></sup>

Among those 255 towns, the median time since the last successful operating override is 16 years. Marblehead's 21 years sits at roughly the 68th percentile. Within this wealthy-suburb cohort, Marblehead is typical, not unusual.

The "Marblehead is unusually override-resistant" framing depends on which ~10 peer towns you pick. The proof in the chart above does not depend on peer selection; it is internal to Marblehead.

## Caveats

- **FY12 and FY22 through FY24 have accounting reclassifications** (GASB pension and other line-item shifts) that produce large year-over-year swings in reported education spending without any real service-level change. The chart handles FY12 with a gap and stops before the FY22-FY24 reclass years.
- **FY20 and FY21 school spending was distorted by COVID-era operational changes** (remote learning, staffing shifts). The clean post-override trend is FY07 through FY19, which is what the analysis above uses.
- **Education is the single largest general-fund category** at about half of spending, and has the longest clean multi-year series. Total general-fund expenditure shows the same pattern, but the GASB reclass swings hit the total as well, making it harder to plot cleanly.
- **Proposition 2&frac12; unused capacity.** A thorough ratcheting test would also check whether the gap between actual levy and levy limit (excess capacity) reappears after an override is absorbed. That requires the DOR <abbr class="g" title="Local Assessment form filed annually with the Department of Revenue">LA-4</abbr> filing's excess-capacity line, which is not yet in this repo.
- **This is not a values question.** Whether the services funded by the early-2000s overrides are worth the $8 million or so of permanent authority they contributed to today's ceiling is a judgment residents will make for themselves. The record shows what happened; it does not say whether that was the right outcome.
