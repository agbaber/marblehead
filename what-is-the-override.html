What Is the Override?

Marblehead is asking voters to approve a Proposition 2.5 operating override to close a projected $8.47 million deficit for fiscal year 2027. The vote is expected in June 2026.

Prop 2.5 is the state law that caps property tax growth at 2.5% per year. An override is the only way to permanently raise taxes beyond that cap. It requires a majority vote at both Town Meeting and at the ballot.

The deficit was forecast a year in advance. The Finance Committee's signed transmittal letter to the spring 2025 Annual Town Meeting told residents a three-year budget forecast had already identified FY27 as a crisis year.

The three-year operating budget forecast "highlighted projected deficits for each of the FY26, FY27, and FY28 periods."

FY26 was balanced only through one-time adjustments, "leading to significant budgetary challenges ahead."

Alec Goolsby, Chair, co-signed by Pat Franklin and Molly Teets, Vice Chairs, Marblehead Finance Committee. Signed transmittal letter, 2026 Finance Committee Report to the Residents of Marblehead (FY26 budget, May 2025 Annual Town Meeting), p. 3.

Nine months later, in January 2026, the Town Administrator presented the annual State of the Town. The first item on the "Biggest Challenges" slide used the same framing.

The "Biggest Challenges" slide listed as its first item a Structural Financial Deficit, driven by an "ongoing gap between recurring revenues and recurring expenditures" and "significant reliance on one-time funds to balance operating budgets."

Thatcher Kezer, Town Administrator, and Aleesha Benjamin, Finance Director, Town of Marblehead. 2026 State of the Town presentation, slide 9, January 28, 2026.

The Three Tiers

The tiers are nested. Tier 2 includes everything in Tier 1. Tier 3 includes everything in Tiers 1 and 2. The highest tier that passes sets the override amount.

Tier 1: Restore ($9 million)

Bring back services cut from the no-override budget

Firefighter position, school resource officer, full library staffing, custodians, groundskeeper, Council on Aging staff, and positions across finance, community development, and other departments.

"This is not a true restore. I've almost called it 'Survive.'"

Dan Fox, Chair, Marblehead Select Board, at the April 8, 2026 Select Board meeting. Quoted in Will Dowd, "Kezer, Benjamin present $9M-to-$15M tiered override plan and separate $2.3M trash tax option," Marblehead Independent, April 8, 2026.

Tier 2: Stabilize ($12 million)

Everything in Tier 1, plus maintenance and staffing

Additional public safety staffing, building maintenance funding, and salary adjustments.

Tier 3: Invest ($15 million)

Everything in Tiers 1 and 2, plus capital

Capital investments totaling roughly $2.1 million across schools, town infrastructure, and maintenance.

Separate Question: Trash ($2.3 million)

Fund curbside trash/recycling through the levy instead of a flat fee

Independent of the tiers. If it fails, the Board of Health will implement a flat fee of roughly $262 per household. Either way, residents pay for trash. The override spreads the cost by home value; the fee is the same for everyone.

The nesting matters more than it looks. At the April 8, 2026 Select Board meeting where the tiered structure was first presented, one resident added the three dollar amounts together and described the ballot as a $36 million ask.

"If you vote for each choice, then you're voting for $36 million."

Ginny O'Brien, resident, at the April 8, 2026 Select Board meeting. Quoted in Will Dowd, "Kezer, Benjamin present $9M-to-$15M tiered override plan and separate $2.3M trash tax option," Marblehead Independent, April 8, 2026.

That reading is arithmetically understandable (9+12+15=36) but the ballot does not work that way. Because the tiers are nested, a yes vote on all three still caps the override at the highest tier that passes, $15 million, not $36 million. If the nested structure tripped up a careful attendee at the meeting where it was first presented, it is likely to confuse voters at the polls.

How You'll Vote

Two ballot questions, expected June 2026.

Question 1: The Override (three parts)

Vote yes or no on each tier you'd accept:

1A: $15,000,000 (Invest)
1B: $12,000,000 (Stabilize)
1C: $9,000,000 (Restore)

The highest tier that gets a majority wins. If you'd accept any override, vote yes on all three. If only 1C passes, you get $9M. If all three pass, you get $15M.

Question 2: Trash (separate yes/no)

Fund curbside trash and recycling through the property tax ($2,298,575). This is independent of Question 1.

What It Costs

The override phases in over three years (FY27 to FY29). Year 1 costs are modest; the full amount hits in Year 3. See the override cost calculator for year-by-year costs at different home values.

History

Marblehead's last successful operating override was in FY2005, 21 years ago. Since 1982: 3 of 21 operating overrides approved (14%). By comparison, 68 of 84 debt exclusions approved (81%). Voters are far more willing to fund capital projects than operating budgets.

The structural problem this override addresses is not new. The Finance Committee first warned residents about unsustainable free cash reliance seven years ago, in a signed transmittal letter to Town Meeting.

"Our continued reliance on Free Cash to balance our budget is not sustainable."

Benjamin S. Berman, Chair, co-signed by Blair Nelson and Emily Belfbecker, Vice Chairs, Marblehead Finance Committee. Signed transmittal letter, 2019 Finance Committee Report to the Residents of Marblehead, p. 2.

Three years later the warning sharpened into a specific prediction. The 2022 transmittal letter told residents an override would likely be required the following year to maintain level services.

"The Town of Marblehead faces a structural budget challenge... Marblehead will likely require an override next year to simply maintain the same Town and School services that residents currently receive."

Alec Goolsby, Chair, co-signed by Emily Belfbecker and C. Pat Franklin, Vice Chairs, Marblehead Finance Committee. Signed transmittal letter, 2022 Finance Committee Report to the Residents of Marblehead, pp. 2-3.

The prediction came true almost verbatim. The following April the Finance Committee formally endorsed a $2,472,056 override, the first such recommendation in eighteen years, repeating the 2022 language with only the tense changed.

"Marblehead needs an override this year to simply maintain the same Town and School services that residents currently receive... for the first time since 2005, the Town is recommending a $2.5 million Proposition 2.5 override... The vote was 9-0 in favor."

Alec Goolsby, Chair, co-signed by Pat Franklin and Cameron Staples, Vice Chairs, Marblehead Finance Committee. Signed transmittal letter, 2023 Finance Committee Report to the Residents of Marblehead (FY24 budget, May 1, 2023 Annual Town Meeting), pp. 2-3.

The override passed Town Meeting 534-230 but lost at the June 20, 2023 ballot 2,992-3,399, a margin of about 400 votes. Organized opposition came from residents and one dissenting Select Board member.

Jack Buba, a former Finance Committee member, wrote a signed letter to the Marblehead Current opposing the override months before the vote.

"As a former Finance Committee member, I lost my position shortly after I voted against pay raises... You live within your budget, make the town do the same."

Jack Buba, former Finance Committee member, Palmer Road. "LETTER: Make town live within its budget," Marblehead Current, February 18, 2023.

Jim Nye, the only Select Board member to oppose the override, read a prepared statement at the League of Women Voters Candidates Night.

"For the past 18 years, the town has paid all its obligations with revenue collected... Most would call that 'conservative fiscal management.' ... I do not support the permanent general override."

Jim Nye, Marblehead Select Board member. Quoted in Kris Olson, "In Select Board field, Nye stands alone as 'no' on override," Marblehead Current, May 24, 2023.

Sue MacInnis, chairwoman of the "Six Percent Is Too Much" ballot committee, warned that passing the override would set a pattern rather than resolve the problem.

"If the override passes it will be overspending as usual. Nothing will change, and we will have yet another override next year."

Sue MacInnis, treasurer and chairwoman, "Six Percent Is Too Much" ballot committee. Quoted in Will Dowd, "ELECTION 2023: Marblehead to decide divisive override on Tuesday," Marblehead Current, June 14, 2023.

The override was defeated. One year later, the Finance Committee's next transmittal letter documented the loss and anticipated a new request.

"For the first time since 2005, the Town requested a proposition 2.5 override... it faced defeat at the polls by a margin of around 400 votes... the Finance Committee anticipates the possibility of such a request being deliberated in the near future."

Alec Goolsby, Chair, co-signed by Pat Franklin and Molly Teets, Vice Chairs, Marblehead Finance Committee. Signed transmittal letter, 2025 Finance Committee Report to the Residents of Marblehead (FY25 budget, May 2024 Annual Town Meeting), p. 4.

At the April 8, 2026 Select Board meeting where the current tiered override was presented, Moses Grader, who had signed the Finance Committee's "eleventh consecutive balanced budget" transmittal letter as FinCom Chair in 2016, reacted to the FY27 numbers.

"There's a real story around how the balanced budget was achieved."

Moses Grader, Marblehead Select Board member (Finance Committee Chair, 2016). Quoted in Will Dowd, "Kezer, Benjamin present $9M-to-$15M tiered override plan and separate $2.3M trash tax option," Marblehead Independent, April 8, 2026.

Key Terms

Prop 2.5 -- State law capping annual property tax growth at 2.5%. Overrides are the only way to exceed this cap permanently.

GIC (Group Insurance Commission) -- State agency that runs health insurance for participating municipalities. Marblehead joined in FY2012. The GIC sets plan designs and rates. The town has no control over either.

PEC (Public Employee Committee) -- Represents all town employee unions and retirees. Any change to the 83/17 health insurance split requires PEC agreement under state law.

Debt exclusion -- A separate type of override for capital projects (e.g., the $8.5M Abbot Library renovation in 2021). Temporary, outside the operating budget, not part of this override.

Free cash -- Surplus from prior years. The town has been using $7-9M of free cash annually to balance the budget. The Finance Committee has warned this is unsustainable.
