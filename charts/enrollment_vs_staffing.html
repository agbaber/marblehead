School Enrollment and Education Staffing

Marblehead Public Schools. From ACFRs and DESE (Massachusetts Department of Elementary and Secondary Education).

Student enrollment (FY01–FY24, from ACFRs)

Student enrollment peaked at 3,327 (FY14) and has declined 21% to 2,617 (FY24). Enrollment from ACFR "Demographic and Economic Statistics" tables.

Three measures of staffing

How much has school staffing changed? The answer depends on which count you use. Two state-reported measures show a decline. One town-reported measure shows an increase. All three are charted below for FY15 to FY24, the period where all sources overlap.

1. ACFR "education FTE" (all town education employees)

From the town's annual financial reports. Includes teachers, paraprofessionals, administrators, custodial, food service, and all other staff the town categorizes under "education." FY15: 490 → FY24: 537 (+9.6%)

2. DESE total educator FTE (school-reported staff)

From the state Department of Elementary and Secondary Education. Includes teachers, co-teachers, paraprofessionals, counselors, psychologists, administrators, and office staff reported by the schools. Does not include custodial or food service. FY15: 501.8 → FY24: 431.8 (−13.9%)

3. DESE classroom teacher FTE

From DESE. Counts only licensed teachers (general education, special education, and English learner). The narrowest of the three measures. FY15: 256.8 → FY24: 245.7 (−4.3%)

Over the same FY15–FY24 period, enrollment fell 19% (3,245 to 2,617). The ACFR measure (chart 1) shows staffing up 9.6%. The two DESE measures (charts 2 and 3) show staffing down 13.9% and 4.3% respectively. The divergence is explained below.

Why the measures disagree

The ACFR and DESE totals were similar in FY15 (490 vs. 502) but diverged sharply by FY24 (537 vs. 432). Two factors explain the gap:

  • Different definitions. The ACFR "Full-time Equivalent Town Employees by Function, education row" is a town-reported number that includes custodial, food service, and other non-educator staff that DESE does not count.
  • A FY23 data discontinuity. The ACFR shows a one-year jump from 483 to 537 education FTE between FY22 and FY23, while DESE shows a decline from 450 to 445 over the same period. The ACFR number then held at exactly 537.0 for two consecutive years, suggesting a rounding or methodology change rather than 54 actual new hires. See the FY23 data jump for the full analysis.

In short: the ACFR number is the broadest count and the one most affected by the FY23 discontinuity. The DESE numbers, reported directly by the schools to the state, show educator staffing declining.

Where did the teacher decline come from?

The 4.3% drop in total teacher FTE masks a composition shift. General education teachers fell 12.2% while special education teachers grew 27.1%:

General education Special education

Teacher FTE by program area, FY15–FY24, from DESE. General education teachers fell from 210.1 to 184.5 (−12.2%). Special education teachers rose from 44.6 to 56.7 (+27.1%). The net result is a small total decline (256.8 to 245.7), but the mix shifted substantially toward mandated special education staffing. Raw data in data/dese_teacher_fte_by_program.csv.

Which measure should I use? It depends on the question. The ACFR number captures the total cost footprint (every education employee the town pays). The DESE total captures school-side staffing (the people working in school buildings). The DESE teacher count captures classroom instruction specifically. Claims about "staffing going up" or "staffing going down" should specify which measure, because two of the three show a decline.

The declining enrollment / flat staffing pattern is common across Massachusetts and is partly driven by special education mandates (IEP-required staffing), out-of-district placements ($4.6M in FY26 school budget), and federal requirements that do not scale with enrollment.
