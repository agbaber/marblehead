Three measures of staffing

How much has school staffing changed? The answer depends on which count you use. All three measures are indexed to FY15 = 100 so they can be compared on the same scale. Click a legend item to toggle it.

Enrollment ACFR education FTE DESE total educator FTE DESE teacher FTE

All four measures indexed to FY15 = 100. Over FY15–FY24, enrollment fell 19%. The ACFR (town financial reports) shows education staffing up 9.6%, but this is the only measure that rises and is driven by a FY23 data discontinuity. The two DESE measures (state education data) show declines: total educator FTE down 13.9%, classroom teacher FTE down 4.3%.

What each measure counts
  • ACFR education FTE (490 → 537): From the town's annual financial reports. Counts all staff the town categorizes under "education," a broader definition than what the state tracks.
  • DESE total educator FTE (501.8 → 431.8): From the state. Includes teachers, co-teachers, paraprofessionals, counselors, psychologists, administrators, and office staff reported by the schools.
  • DESE teacher FTE (256.8 → 245.7): From the state. Only licensed classroom teachers (general education, special education, and English learner).

Why the measures disagree

The ACFR and DESE totals were similar in FY15 (490 vs. 502) but diverged sharply by FY24 (537 vs. 432). The main driver is a single event:

  • The FY23 data discontinuity. The ACFR jumped from 483 to 537 education FTE between FY22 and FY23, while DESE shows a decline from 450 to 445 over the same period. The ACFR number then held at exactly 537.0 for two consecutive years. That pattern, a sudden jump contradicted by the state data and then frozen at a round number, points to a counting methodology change rather than 54 actual new hires. Without this jump, the ACFR would show roughly flat staffing, and all three measures would point in the same direction. See the FY23 data jump for the full analysis.

The two sources also define "education staff" differently. The ACFR uses a broader definition of "education staff" than DESE, though exactly which positions it includes beyond what DESE tracks is not broken out in the report. This explains why you should not expect the two numbers to match at any given point, but it does not explain why the trends point in opposite directions. The FY23 jump does.

Where did the teacher decline come from?

The 4.3% drop in total teacher FTE masks a composition shift. General education teachers fell 12.2% while special education teachers grew 27.1%:

General education Special education

Teacher FTE by program area, FY15–FY24, from DESE. General education teachers fell from 210.1 to 184.5 (−12.2%). Special education teachers rose from 44.6 to 56.7 (+27.1%). The net result is a small total decline (256.8 to 245.7), but the mix shifted substantially toward mandated special education staffing. Raw data in data/dese_teacher_fte_by_program.csv.

Which measure should I use? It depends on the question. The ACFR number captures the total cost footprint (every education employee the town pays). The DESE total captures school-side staffing (the people working in school buildings). The DESE teacher count captures classroom instruction specifically. Claims about "staffing going up" or "staffing going down" should specify which measure, because two of the three show a decline.

The declining enrollment / flat staffing pattern is common across Massachusetts and is partly driven by special education mandates (IEP-required staffing), out-of-district placements ($4.6M in FY26 school budget), and federal requirements that do not scale with enrollment.
