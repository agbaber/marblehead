Override Contribution and Underlying Revenue

Two views of the same FY2002–FY2025 window. The top panel isolates what each peer town has added to its permanent levy base through passed Proposition 2½ operating overrides. The bottom panel strips those overrides out and shows how each town's underlying revenue grew from its FY2002 base.

The question these two panels together answer: is the difference between Marblehead's revenue trajectory and its peers' revenue trajectories driven by override ballot activity, or is it baked into the underlying revenue base? If peer lines in the bottom panel converge with Marblehead's, the divergence is about overrides. If peer lines stay above Marblehead's, peers are outpacing on fundamentals too (new growth, state aid, local receipts, economic revaluation) and overrides are only part of the story.

Panel A. Override-added levy authority

Cumulative permanent levy authority added through passed operating overrides, compounded at 2.5% per year from the win date. Expressed as a percent of each town's FY2002 property tax levy.

Panel B. Non-override revenue growth

Actual general fund revenue minus the override authority from Panel A, indexed so FY2002 equals zero. If peer lines converge toward Marblehead's, divergence is explained by overrides; if they stay above, peers are outpacing on fundamentals.

Notes, methodology, sources

Panel A calculation. For each passed operating override, the dollar amount voted onto the permanent levy base is carried forward at 2.5% per year through FY2025 (the Proposition 2½ growth cap). The running total is divided by the town's FY2002 tax levy from DLS Schedule A. Only ballot questions of vote-type "Override" that passed ("WIN") with fiscal year between FY2002 and FY2025 inclusive. Debt exclusions, capital exclusions, and stabilization transfers are not counted.

Panel B calculation. For each town and year, actual total general fund revenue (DLS Schedule A, Revenues view, "Total Revenues" column) minus the cumulative override authority computed in Panel A. The result is divided by the town's FY2002 total general fund revenue to express growth as a percent of FY2002. A peer town whose Panel B line matches Marblehead's had the same underlying revenue growth as Marblehead, and the divergence visible in raw revenue comparisons comes entirely from overrides.

Why subtract override authority, not every dollar of levy growth. The override amount is the specific addition to permanent levy authority from voter action. Normal levy growth (2.5% plus new growth plus state aid and local receipts) is part of the underlying trajectory we want to compare. Subtracting just the override authority isolates voter-driven levy additions from organic revenue growth.

What this does not measure. Actual tax levy, which depends on unused levy capacity decisions and debt-exclusion overrides that do not add to the permanent base. Operating expenditures or service levels. Whether a town needed the override or used the money well. Those are separate questions.

Data gap. Marblehead's FY2025 DLS Schedule A has not yet been filed, so Marblehead's Panel B line stops at FY2024. All other peer towns extend through FY2025.

Source. Massachusetts Division of Local Services, Proposition 2½ Override and Debt Exclusion databank, accessed FY2026. FY2002 tax levy and total revenue values from MA DLS Schedule A General Fund.
